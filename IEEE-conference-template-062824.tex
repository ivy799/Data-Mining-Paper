\begin{abstract}
Landslides are one of the most frequent natural disasters and often pose serious threats and significant losses. Although most studies focus on external triggers such as rainfall and slope inclination, this study investigates the influence of internal factors, namely soil characteristics, on landslide risk. This study examines the role of the properties of the topsoil and subsoil layers in landslide risk and develops a predictive model using data mining techniques. Here, we compiled and introduced a new integrated dataset built by combining three main data sources: the Harmonized World Soil Database (HWSD), the Global Landslide Catalog (GLC), and the administrative boundaries from Natural Earth. First, a comparative statistical analysis was conducted to confirm significant differences in soil properties between landslide and non-landslide areas. Next, the XGBoost algorithm was applied to model landslide risk. The results showed that models using the topsoil layer, subsoil layer, and a combination of both produced consistent AUC values of around 0.73. However, F1 score analysis revealed that subsoil characteristics, particularly chemical composition and texture, had slightly higher predictive power than topsoil. The combined model, which integrated both layers, achieved an F1 score of 0.71, an increase of about 3\%. These findings indicate that although the characteristics of the subsoil layer are slightly more dominant, information from the topsoil layer provides an essential complementary contribution to achieving optimal model performance.
\end{abstract}
